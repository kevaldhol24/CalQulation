It was late 2023, and I was sitting at my kitchen table surrounded by printouts, a calculator, and three different Excel spreadsheets. My wife looked over my shoulder and asked what I was doing.

"Trying to figure out if we should prepay part of our home loan or increase our SIP investments," I replied, not looking up.

"And how long have you been at it?" she asked.

"Just about... four hours now," I admitted.

That moment—that frustration of trying to make an informed financial decision without proper tools—was when the idea for Calqulation was born. Not as a business plan or startup pitch, but as a genuine need. I simply wanted a calculator that understood how Indians save, invest, and manage debt.

### The Breaking Point: One Calculation Too Many

I've always considered myself financially savvy. As someone who grew up in a middle-class household in Ahmedabad where every rupee was accounted for, I learned early on that financial decisions matter. By 2023, I had a good job in tech, a home loan from a leading bank, and a disciplined investment routine.

Yet despite my technical background and financial discipline, I struggled with what should have been straightforward decisions:

- Should I prepay my home loan or increase my SIP?
- How would rising interest rates affect my floating rate loan over its 20-year term?
- Was I saving enough for my parents' healthcare needs, considering India's medical inflation?
- How would the new tax regime affect my investment strategy?

The existing calculators were either too simplistic or completely disconnected from Indian realities. Most were built for Western financial systems and failed to account for our unique challenges.

<PullQuote author="Co-founder" role="Former Financial Analyst">
  When we started, we weren't thinking about building a product for the market. We were simply solving our own problems. Every feature in our calculator exists because one of us personally needed it at some point.
</PullQuote>

He was right. Most tools couldn't account for things like planning for a sibling's education, regular remittances to parents in another city, or saving for children's weddings alongside retirement.

### From Spreadsheets to Prototype: The Early Days

What began as a personal spreadsheet soon evolved. I shared it with a few colleagues who faced similar challenges. One of them, now our CTO, suggested turning it into a proper application.

"People need this," he insisted. "Not just tech professionals like us, but everyone from a young professional in Bangalore to a small business owner in Lucknow."

We started meeting on weekends, expanding the spreadsheet into something more powerful. Our first prototype was crude—just a web form connected to our calculation engine—but it worked. It could handle:

- Home loan optimizations with Indian prepayment rules
- SIP calculations with step-up options tied to expected salary growth
- Tax benefit comparisons between investment options
- Inflation-adjusted goal planning

When I showed it to my father, he spent three hours on it, recalculating his retirement plans. "This," he said, "actually makes sense to me."

The solution came in the form of a layered interface—simple on the surface with the option to "go deeper" into more sophisticated parameters.

<PullQuote author="Our Lead Developer" role="Financial Technology Specialist">

</PullQuote>
